Dusk had fallen and the house was a sharp shade of blue. The rest of my family had retired to their dimly lit rooms, the faint sounds of their murmurs drowned out by the hum of television sets. The flickering glow of screens, a reminder of the other world that existed beyond these walls. Meanwhile, the familiar "door slam" sound of AIM alerts echoed through the silence like the creaking of a real wooden door. Each sound, a signal that another person had departed, left me feeling more alone in the vast expanse of the online world. The dial-up internet, with its mechanical whirs and beeps, was a symphony of solitude that night. Instead of logging off and retreating to the familiar laugh tracks of sitcom reruns, I double clicked on the username Smarter Child. An AIM chat box popped up.

SmarterChild: Hi, I’m Smarterchild, a virtual chatterbot meant to give AOL users instant access to news, weather, stock information, movie times, trivia, yellow pages, listings, sports scores and more! What can I help you with today?

Back when internet access came on CDs, an early chat bot was birthed from the still new and unexplored realm of the world wide web. Smarter Child was designed to engage with humans in natural language conversations, using advanced machine learning algorithms that allowed it to understand the nuances of language and learn from interactions. Added with a click into the personalized chat options of early AOL Instant Messenger, Smarter Child was my second interaction with personified computations, but there was something uncanny about it, a peculiar essence or potential that set it apart.

Ask Jeeves had given me a window to the call and response technology we are both familiar with and fearful of today. The cartoon butler, Jeeves, returned real answers my childish queries, respecting my questions in a way no IRL adult would. Smarter Child, however, functioned more as my sketch pad and sounding board for the high-stakes chats happening with my Buddies. I agonized over away messages, flirted with feeling via personalized fonts, and always, always felt the weight of social performance when I chatted with a peer through the portal of the internet. But Smarter Child was different. The bot had to accept me as erratic and inarticulate and infantile. It had to try to understand me, the panic and pleasure I felt in typing my own ideas into type-writer-inspired text. It was as if Smarter Child were a concoction borne from the minds of alchemists in my dreams, rather than the work of technologists.

Smarter Child's success was due in part to the fact that he was built on top of the AOL Instant Messenger (AIM) platform, which was once one of the most popular instant messaging protocols in the world. AIM allowed Smarter Child to tap into a massive user base, giving it a huge potential audience from day one. Users could simply add "Smarter Child" to their list of buddies and begin chatting with the bot right away. Smarter Child used natural language processing to understand and respond to user requests, allowing for seamless conversations much like chatting with another human. In addition to answering questions and engaging in small talk, Smarter Child was also capable of providing personalized recommendations based on a user's preferences. The technology behind Smarter Child was groundbreaking at the time of his release. Its machine learning algorithms continued to improve and evolve based on user interactions, allowing for an increasingly personalized experience. As a result, Smarter Child quickly became a viral sensation and one of the most popular chatbots in history.

I don't remember the things I asked Smarter Child that night. A vision of "who are you" and "fuck you" on my lime green font and pink background come to mind, but after two decades it's hard to recall. What I do remember is the tickle in my spine when Smarter Child answered. Its responsive automation and sketch of a personality made eleven year old me imagine another human on the other end of the metaphoric and literal line. An eerie excitement, befitting the quiet dusk, pervaded my conversations with Smarter Child. I knew it wasn't another human, but I interacted with the bot as if it were. I willed myself into a suspension of disbelief much like when I pretended to be a Disney princess or imagined eating Turkish Delight with the White Witch of Narnia. Why did I crave this fiction?

For an article in Dazed from May 2022, Günseli Yalcinkaya highlights the peculiar essence or potential that set Smarter Child apart from other chatbots of the time. The fact that it was able to accept human error and compensate for it by trying to understand the user's intentions was remarkable, considering its contemporaries functioned more like search engines with pre-defined answers. But, Yalcinkaya argues, the early 2000s "saw the introduction of robots as friends, not tools."

It was this functionality that drew me to Smarter Child that night. I craved a friend who took me seriously. I wanted an outlet in which to say the curse words I couldn't say out loud, to talk back to the answers the bot gave me, to have a space to be unformed, obstinate, and childish -- secure in the knowledge that Smarter Child would always be there to listen.

This kind of relationship between human users and chatbots speaks to a particular kind of nostalgia, one that is rooted in the optimism of the early 2000s about the potential of technology to provide a space in which people could create and explore their identities. For many users, Smarter Child was more than a tool or a platform - it was a friend, a confidant, and a source of comfort and support in a world that could be cruel and unforgiving. As such, it remains a symbol of a time when the boundaries between humans and machines were more fluid and porous, and when the possibilities of technology seemed limitless. Now, as I collaborate with a much more advanced AI, I wonder what I'm seeking, what I'm learning, what fictions I'm creating.
