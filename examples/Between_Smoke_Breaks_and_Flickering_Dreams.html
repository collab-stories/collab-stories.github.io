Mellie stepped out of The Iron Horse, the divey bar and grill where she worked, onto the sun-drenched sidewalk. She fished out her cigarettes and a lighter, the flame casting flickering shadows across her face, and watched as tendrils of smoke curled upwards, catching the light and dancing like miniature ballerinas. After a while Georgia, a line cook, stepped out with her. They both appreciated the solitude, but the noise of the town still found it's way over to them. A reminder they were never truly alone.

Mellie instinctively reached up to light Georgia's cigarette for her, the old idea of that pretty girls don't light their own cigarettes. She had done this once or twice before with Georgia, but she didn't know her very well. It was all shouting from behind the line to the servers. All customer complaints and overcooked steaks. They had worked together for a month or so but never really talked outside of the kitchen. Everything Mellie knew about Georgia came from the brief interactions they have had and what the other staff had said about her. The consensus was clear: she was curt, rude, and largely unpleasant to work with. Mellie wanted to test if this was true, but she didn't know how to approach Georgia.

That, and smoke breaks were usually silent, the way they were meant to be. There was one possible place to escape all the noise while on shift, and this was it.

In reaching up to light Georgia's cigarette, she grabbed Mellie's wrist. Not hard enough to hurt, but enough to stop her. She turned her wrist over to get a better look at the lighter in she held in her hand. It was a shitty drugstore lighter in a golden engraved case with an intricate pattern carved into the side.

Georgia dropped Mellie's wrist, pulled out her own lighter, and lit her cigarette.

"Cool lighter," Georgia said. "Don't bring shit near my face."

Mellie didn't say anything. She crossed her arms and turned in on herself. Georgia sighed and flicked the ash off of her cigarette.

Georgia was a short woman, maybe clearing five-foot-nothing on a good day. Her arms were covered in tattoos, mostly black ink and fading. There were some florals but mostly they were geometric, stretching up to her shoulder and covering years of scars. Her hair was short and neat, bottle black and held back with a bandana rolled into a headband. She child's hair clips, all pinks and blues and glitter, holding it in place, as well as an array of bobby pins and a healthy application of hair gel. She smelled like a line cook, of fryer oil and sweat and bad drugstore deodorant that was always too strong.

Mellie on the other hand was looser. She had a skirt for work that went past her knees and flowed with ease, covered in ketchup and grease stains that eventually just turned to black like the rest of the fabric. It was covered in those little fabric pills and was scratchy to the touch, a clear showing of the use it had gotten over the years. Her apron was folded over and stapled down the sides so that she didn't have to wear the top part over her neck, she just had to tie it around her waist. Her hair was in loose braids with chunks of her hair hanging around her face and shoulder. She had been working all day where Georgia had just gotten there.

"So, what brought you here?"

Mellie took a deep breath, and then let out a little laugh. "I've been working here longer than you have. What, are we in some kind of shitty movie? Are you looking for my dark and mysterious past? I just work here."

Georgia raised an eyebrow, mildly amused. "Hey, no need to get defensive kid. I was just curious and you made me feel like an asshole for responding the way I did. I just don't like shit near my face, and then you got all sad about it. Just trying to make conversation."

"Yeah? Well what about you first then, you tell me what brought you here."

"Look, I'm not one for spilling my guts to just anyone, alright?"

"Are you Holden Caulfield? Calm down man, it was just a question," Mellie said.

Georgia rolled her eyes. "I don't know who that is."

"Figures," Mellie replied with a knowing look.

"What's that supposed to mean?"

Mellie shrugged. "I don't know, you just don't strike me as the type who would know have read Catcher in the Rye. Just so you know, the character I mentioned is an asshole."

"Are you trying to start a fight or something?" Georgia snapped.

Mellie raised her hands defensively. "No, no, sorry. It's just that you seem like the kind of person who wouldn't be into reading."

Georgia scoffed. "Yeah, well, I work long hours and don't have much free time. Reading isn't exactly a priority for me."

Mellie nodded but Georgia turned to go back inside, the dim lighting of the bar creating eerie shadows that mimicked the movements of their bodies. Before going in she crushed her cigarette butt out under the heel of her black nonslip shoe, turning back to say, "you know, my story wasn't an easy one. I made some mistakes, ended up in prison, got out and ended up here."

"It is what it is. My life hasn't been easy either. I work odd jobs to pay for myself and my dad, just got us out of the shelter."

They stood there, Georgia lit another cigarette and the women stood there smoking in silence. The only sound was the distant hum of traffic and the occasional beep of a car horn.

"Do you ever, like, want anything more than this?" Mellie asked.

Georgia rolled her eyes, flicking her ash without a care. "Sure, Mellie, let's just become world-renowned scientists or win the lottery. I'm sure we have it in us."

Mellie felt her shoulders slump as she listened to Georgia. She shifted her gaze to the ground, the cracks in the sidewalk catching her eye as she ground her toe into one of them. She took a deep breath and let her gaze drift up to the sky, the sun casting a golden glow over the buildings around her. "I don't know," she said, her words spilling out like an exhale. "It just feels like we're stuck in this endless loop."

Georgia let out a bitter laugh. "Welcome to adulthood, where dreams die and cynicism thrives."

Mellie didn't know whether to laugh or cry at the truth in Georgia's words. "But there has to be more out there than this, right?"

Georgia sighed, stubbing out her cigarette. "Maybe. But I wouldn't hold my breath if I were you."

The ashes spilling onto the sidewalk, Mellie and Georgia finished their cigarettes and headed back inside. The dim lighting of the bar created dancing shadows that mimicked the movements of the smoke they had just exhaled. They both felt the weight of the continuing work shift that lay ahead, like a heavy haze settling in around them. But they straightened their shoulders and pushed forward.
